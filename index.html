About

I am a third-year PhD candidate in Economics at the University of Arkansas. My research sits at the intersection of development, labor, and health economics — examining how early-life exposures, healthcare policy, and industrial shocks shape long-run human capital and welfare outcomes in low-income settings, with a primary focus on Bangladesh.

I am currently a Distinguished Doctoral Fellow and a Research Assistant on a USDA-funded nutrition and machine vision project. I also teach undergraduate economics.

Research
Working Paper

Paying for Birth: Do Vouchers Raise Facility Delivery and Reduce Mortality? Evidence from Bangladesh

with Md Amzad Hossain and Moin Uddin Ahmed

Finds a 27 percent reduction in stillbirths using a staggered difference-in-differences design across 51 subdistricts, with the largest effects concentrated among poor and urban households.

Work in Progress

Early-Life Arsenic Exposure and Long-Run Labor Market Outcomes: Evidence from a Staggered Mitigation Campaign in Bangladesh JMP

Advisor: Md Amzad Hossain

Uses a triple-difference design exploiting a UNICEF mitigation campaign rollout across 45 subdistricts to estimate whether early-life arsenic exposure reduction generates adult labor market gains in the long run.

Work in Progress

Industrial Disruption and Women's Lives: Evidence from the Rana Plaza Collapse in Bangladesh

Advisor: Md Amzad Hossain

Examines how post-2013 garment sector restructuring affected women's marriage, fertility, autonomy, and domestic violence outcomes using a local labor market exposure design.

Work in Progress

The Effect of Coastal Climate Resilient Infrastructure Project (CCRIP) on Women in Environmentally Vulnerable Areas in Bangladesh
